    The Political Echo-Chamber Is Not a New Phenomenon: What the 2004 Political Blogosphere Can Teach Us Today

    Has there been a time where America was more divided than it is today? The 2016 U.S. Presidential Elections showed that voters were extremely divided along party lines, with each side so at odds with the other that there seemed to be no conceivable way to bridge the gap. Why has it come down to this? In the 2016 elections, the liberals and conservatives both seemed to be living in separate narratives of their own creation. At its extremes, right-wing media network Breitbart propagated a conspiracy theory, now dubbed Pizzagate, that Hillary Clinton was sexually abusing children in satanic rituals in the basement of a pizza restaurant in Washington, D.C. If you think that is preposterous, 46% of Trump voters believe the theory to be true, or that it is possibly true.

    Why is this so? Today, research has shown that there is almost no overlap in what conservatives and liberals deem as trustworthy media sources. This essentially means that the sources from which liberals and conservatives gather information, reason, and make decisions are completely different. We live in an echo chamber where we are exposed only to opinions that agree with our own. This hampers the deliberative process in democracies because people, purposefully or as a result of their social environment, never have to contend or reason with opposing perspectives—much less attempt to find a compromise.
